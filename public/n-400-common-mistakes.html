The N-400 naturalization application is 20 pages long with hundreds of questions. One wrong answer, one missing document, or one overlooked detail can delay your application by months — or get it denied. These are the 10 mistakes we see most often.

THE 10 MISTAKES

MISTAKE 01
Not Disclosing Every Trip Outside the U.S.
The N-400 asks about every trip outside the United States — even a weekend in Canada or Mexico, even a day trip. Most people forget short trips or only list the long ones. USCIS cross-references your passport stamps and entry/exit records. If your list doesn't match their records, it raises a red flag about your honesty.
THE FIX
Go through every passport page and list every stamp. Check your credit card statements for foreign charges. Think about every holiday, family visit, or work trip. When in doubt — include it.
MISTAKE 02
Leaving Questions Blank Instead of Writing "N/A"
Many applicants leave questions blank when they don't apply to them. USCIS interprets blank questions as incomplete applications — which triggers a Request for Evidence (RFE) or immediate rejection. This adds months to your timeline.
THE FIX
For any question that doesn't apply to you, write "N/A" (not applicable). Never leave any field blank. If a question asks for dates and none apply, write "N/A" in the date fields too.
MISTAKE 03
Not Disclosing Past Legal Issues
This is the most dangerous mistake. People hide arrests, citations, DUIs, or even minor infractions thinking they won't be found. USCIS runs a full FBI background check on every applicant. They will find everything. Hiding a past issue and having USCIS discover it is far worse than disclosing it upfront — it's considered misrepresentation, which can permanently bar you from citizenship.
THE FIX
Disclose everything, even charges that were dismissed, expunged, or that you received a pardon for. If you have any criminal history, consult an immigration attorney before filing.
MISTAKE 04
Using the Wrong Name
Your N-400 must use your exact legal name as it appears on your green card — not a nickname, not a shortened version, not a name you go by at work. Even small differences like "Mike" vs "Michael" or missing a middle name can cause problems.
THE FIX
Copy your name exactly from your green card, character by character. If you want to officially change your name as part of naturalization, there's a specific section on the N-400 for that.
MISTAKE 05
Not Filing Tax Returns
USCIS requires that you've filed federal income taxes for the past 5 years (or that you weren't required to). Many immigrants don't realize this is a requirement, or they filed late, or they filed incorrectly. Missing tax returns are one of the top reasons for application delays.
THE FIX
Get copies of your last 5 years of federal tax returns from IRS.gov. If you missed a year, file it now before submitting your N-400. The IRS late filing penalty is much smaller than missing your citizenship window.
MISTAKE 06
Sending Original Documents Instead of Copies
USCIS asks you to submit copies of supporting documents — not originals. Many people send their original green card, original birth certificate, or original tax returns. USCIS may lose documents or take months to return them.
THE FIX
Always send clear photocopies. Keep all originals at home in a safe place. Only bring originals to your interview — never mail them.
MISTAKE 07
Forgetting to Sign the Application
The N-400 requires multiple signatures in specific places. An unsigned application is automatically rejected and returned to you — costing weeks of delay and requiring you to refile.
THE FIX
After completing the N-400, go through the entire form one more time checking every signature box. There are signatures required in Part 13 (certification), and in some cases additional certifications.
MISTAKE 08
Filing to the Wrong Address
USCIS has different filing addresses depending on your state and whether you're filing by mail or using a courier service. Filing to the wrong address means your application gets returned or lost — and you lose your filing date.
THE FIX
Always check the current filing address at USCIS.gov before mailing. Filing addresses change periodically. Use the USCIS website on the day you mail, not a cached or old version.
MISTAKE 09
Not Preparing for the Interview
Many applicants assume the interview will be easy and walk in unprepared. The civics test, English test, and personal questions all require preparation. Failing the civics test at your interview means you must return for a second interview — adding months to your timeline.
THE FIX
Study all 100 civics questions until you can answer any of them confidently. Practice your English reading and writing. Review your N-400 answers so you can speak to everything on the form.
MISTAKE 10
Applying Too Early
Most applicants must be a permanent resident for 5 years (or 3 years if married to a U.S. citizen). However, you CAN apply up to 90 days before you reach that anniversary. Some people apply too early and their application gets rejected — and they lose the filing fee.
THE FIX
Calculate your 5-year (or 3-year) anniversary date from your green card. You can file starting 90 days before that date — no earlier. When in doubt, wait an extra week.
